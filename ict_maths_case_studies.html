Three Classrooms, Three Decisions

You'll work through three cases drawn from primary schools. Each asks a different question — not "what is the right answer?" but "what kind of thinking does Easingwood & Williams's framework demand here?"

Case 01 / Diagnose

Mr Dhillon's graphing lesson

Year 4 · 60 minutes · ICT suite booked

Mr Dhillon's mathematics coordinator has asked him to "use the ICT suite more" in his data-handling unit. He's planning a lesson on bar charts for next Tuesday. Here is what he's written so far:

Lesson plan — draft 1
Objective:Pupils will produce a bar chart in Microsoft Excel showing favourite fruits in the class.
Starter:Demonstrate on the interactive whiteboard how to enter data into cells A1:B6 and click Insert → Chart.
Main:Pupils copy the same data from the board into Excel and reproduce the chart. Those who finish early will change the chart colour.
Assessment:I will mark pupils on whether they successfully produced a bar chart in Excel by the end of the session.
Differentiation:Higher-attaining pupils will be asked to use 3D bar chart style. Lower-attaining pupils will sit next to a confident ICT user.

Mr Dhillon is pleased — it's ambitious, uses technology, and every child will produce an artefact. His mentor has concerns. What has he got wrong?

Your task

Select every flaw in Mr Dhillon's plan that Easingwood & Williams's framework would identify. Then write a short recommendation for one flaw of your choosing.
Four of the six statements below are genuine flaws. Two are red herrings. Read carefully — the plan is wrong in subtle ways, not obvious ones.
Case 1 debrief

The deeper problem under all four flaws

Mr Dhillon's lesson fails the value-added test. The computer is being used as a display tool, not as a cognitive tool. Pupils replicate a chart that already exists; they do not sort, search, question, predict, or interpret. The assessment, differentiation, and lack of practical work all flow from an objective that centres the software rather than the mathematics.

A fix at the objective level reshapes everything else: replace "produce a bar chart in Excel" with something like "pupils interpret and justify claims from a data set they have collected". The computer now earns its place — speed and provisionality let pupils trial multiple chart types and argue which one best supports their claim.

Sample recommendation (for flaw 1)
Rewrite the objective as a mathematics objective: "Pupils will interpret a bar chart they have constructed to answer a question about their own class." The software becomes the tool for answering the question rather than the thing being taught. Mr Dhillon could then open the lesson with a genuine question ("Which fruit should the tuck shop stock more of?"), let pupils collect data themselves, and use Excel's speed to trial two or three chart types so they can discuss which best supports a recommendation.

Case 02 / Decide

Ms Okafor's LOGO unit

Year 3 · 6-lesson unit · Mixed prior experience

Ms Okafor is planning a six-lesson LOGO unit for her Year 3 class. The mathematics focus is on angle, particularly right angles and their combinations. Her class is mixed — a handful of pupils used a Bee-Bot in Year 1, but most have no programming experience. The school has one set of Roamers, a computer lab, and a site licence for Imagine Logo.

She needs to make four planning decisions. You will guide each one.

Plan so far
Decision 1 of 4

Where should the unit begin?

Given mixed prior experience and the cognitive challenge the authors describe, what is Ms Okafor's best opening lesson?
Why this matters The authors argue that pupils often misread screen-turtle movement as "up/down" rather than "forward/back" when they haven't first experienced direction in a three-dimensional, embodied way. The human turtle (or floor turtle) is the cheap insurance policy against this misconception — skip it and some pupils will carry a conceptual confusion through the rest of the unit.
Decision 2 of 4

How will pupils work at the computer?

Ms Okafor has booked the ICT suite for lessons 3–6. How should she organise pupils across the computers?
Why this matters The authors repeatedly frame LOGO as a vehicle for collaborative learning and discourse. Pairs encourage negotiation ("no, we need to turn more than 90°"); solo work silences that discussion. Mixing experience isn't charity for the less confident — it gives the more confident pupil the cognitively valuable task of explaining, which deepens their own understanding.
Decision 3 of 4

Handling a pupil's question about circles

In lesson 4, a pupil wants to draw a clock face and asks: "How do I make the turtle draw a circle?" Ms Okafor has seen this coming. What is her best response, given the angle focus of her unit?
Why this matters The authors acknowledge the circle question is philosophically contested — sometimes a built-in command is fine. But the governing question is always what is the learning objective? Here the unit's focus is angle, so a REPEAT procedure whose turn must total 360° is the mathematical gold. The pupil's clock face becomes a vehicle for discovering that a full turn equals 360° and that the relationship between step-size and turn-size determines circle size.
Decision 4 of 4

Designing the end-of-unit assessment

Ms Okafor needs evidence of mathematical learning for the unit. Which assessment approach best fits the framework?
Why this matters The Crompton and Mann warning bites hardest at the assessment stage. A computer-based LOGO assessment risks measuring typing speed, mouse accuracy, and familiarity with the software interface — none of which is mathematics. A paper prediction-and-justification task strips away the technology confound and asks directly: can this pupil reason about angle and distance? The best unit-level design pairs something like option A with formative observation (option D) during the unit itself.
Case 2 debrief

The four decisions add up to one principle

Each decision you made tested a different facet of the same idea: keep the mathematical cognition with the learner. Starting concrete protects against misconception. Pairing enables the talk that makes reasoning visible. Scaffolding the circle turns a pupil's aside into the unit's central mathematical moment. Paper-based assessment confirms the learning without the technology confounding it.

Ms Okafor's plan isn't "better" because it uses more technology — it's better because the technology stays in its proper role as a tool, and the child stays in the role of mathematician.

Case 03 / Compare

The Year 5 data-handling planning meeting

Three teachers · Same objective · Different plans

Three Year 5 teachers at Oakfield Primary are planning the same data-handling objective: "Pupils will frame a question, collect data, and use a database to interpret their findings." Each has a different plan. The year group leader has asked them to settle on one approach.

Read all three plans carefully. They are all plausible — no plan is obviously wrong. Your job is to judge which most fully realises Easingwood & Williams's framework, and to justify your choice.

weakest
Ms Harriet
Plan A
QuestionWhich British bird is most common in our area?
DataUse the pre-built British Birds database (43 records).
ActivityPupils in pairs write six questions they can answer by sorting/searching the database. Teacher circulates.
SoftwareInformation Workshop — an open-ended data tool.
AssessmentPupils submit their six questions and the answers they found.
best fit
Mr Jonas
Plan B
QuestionShould the school add more bird feeders? Where?
DataPupils spend one lesson outdoors with clipboards tallying birds seen at three playground locations, then enter their own data.
ActivityPairs frame their own questions from the data they collected, sort/filter to answer them, and produce a one-paragraph recommendation for the head teacher.
SoftwareInformation Workshop, plus the pre-built British Birds database as a comparison reference.
AssessmentThe recommendation paragraph — assessed on whether the claim is supported by the data, not on software fluency.
middle
Ms Priya
Plan C
QuestionTeacher-selected questions of increasing difficulty about British birds.
DataUse the pre-built British Birds database.
ActivityPupils work through a structured worksheet of 12 questions, using increasingly complex search operators (equal to, includes, greater than).
SoftwareInformation Workshop.
AssessmentMarked worksheet, with a short extension task: pupils write one question of their own and the search strategy to answer it.

Your task

Select the plan you think best realises the framework, then write a justification that explains why — and what the other two plans get wrong, or merely get less right.
A strong justification names specific concepts from the chapters (value-added, practical element, assessment focus, software taxonomy, pupil cognition). A weak one just says one plan "is more engaging".
Case 3 analysis

Why Plan B is the strongest fit

All three plans use genuinely conjectural software and a legitimate mathematical topic. The distinction is in who is doing the cognitive work.

Comparison analysis

Plan B (Mr Jonas) is the strongest. It is the only plan where pupils do the full cycle the authors describe — frame a question, collect raw data practically, analyse it with the tool, and interpret the findings into a defensible claim. The computer's speed and sorting power earn their place because there's real data to wrestle with. The assessment targets the mathematics (is the claim data-supported?) rather than the software skill.

Plan A (Ms Harriet) is close, but skips the practical data collection. Pupils work on someone else's data, so the authentic sequence of hypothesising → observing → recording is compressed to just the analysis phase. It's still conjectural use of the software, but the mathematics is thinner.

Plan C (Ms Priya) looks rigorous because of its structure, but the structure is the giveaway: the teacher has framed all the questions, the worksheet prescribes the search strategy, and the "extension" task hints at what was missing from the main task. Well-intentioned, but it slides conjectural software toward a revelatory use — the pupils click through a guided tour rather than investigate.

Key distinction: the same software can be used conjecturally or quasi-revelatorily depending on whether the learner frames the question. That's the hinge the framework turns on.

Three cases, one principle

Across diagnosis, decision-making, and comparison, the same question kept surfacing: does the technology keep the learner's mathematical cognition active, or quietly take it over? That single question covers most of what the authors have to say about planning.

What to carry into your own planning

  • Write the objective as a mathematics objective. If it reads as a software skill, the lesson will drift.
  • Protect the practical element — the computer enhances hands-on mathematics, it doesn't replace it.
  • Choose the software for what the pupil will do with it, not for what it does. The same tool can be conjectural or quasi-revelatory depending on who frames the question.
  • Assess the mathematics, not the mouse. If your assessment could be passed by a fluent typist with no understanding, redesign it.
  • Plan the collaboration. Pair structure, pupil grouping, and the teacher's questions determine whether the discourse is mathematical or procedural.
